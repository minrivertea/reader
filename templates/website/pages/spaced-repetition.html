Spaced Repetition

Best described as a technique that maximises learning whilst minimising the time you need to spend, we also like to call it learning for lazy people.

Think back to school and you probably remember cramming the night before exams, long and repetitive speeches from teachers, and ultimately forgetting most of what you learned.

Effective memory learning depends on correctly controlling when and what you learn. You might have seen something like this diagram before:

It describes how the best time to learn something is just as you're about to forget it and no sooner/later. This is the essence of spaced repetition.

A basic Spaced Repetition process is simple:

  1. You learn something.
  2. A short time later, you review it again to refresh your memory.
  3. A slightly longer time later, you try to remember it (eg. in a test, you're forced to recall the item).
  4. If you fail the test, then you review the item again. If you pass the test, you reschedule a new test, but this time extend the interval.

Why use spaced repetition?

  • The chief benefit is to save time. You optimise your study to avoid excessive repetition, and only learn when it's most efficient to do so.
  • It actually helps! Often the way we normally study encourages bingeing, which strengthens short-term memorisation, but doesn't really help with long term memory.
  • You can learn more! By avoiding study binges or constantly relearning things you've already mastered, you free up time to learn new things.
